# Character Vocabulary Guide
## Who Knows What — Linguistic Authenticity for Each Character

This guide ensures each character uses circus terminology appropriate to their background, status, and experience level. Characters should NOT all sound the same.

---

## CHARACTER PROFILES

### TOMMY BRENDAN (Protagonist)
**Status:** First-of-May → Trouper (by end of novel)  
**Background:** Small-town Texas, trumpet player, college dropout  
**Arc:** Learns circus world through the season

#### Vocabulary Evolution by Chapter

| Chapters | Knowledge Level | Terms He Uses | Terms He Learns |
|----------|----------------|---------------|-----------------|
| 1-2 | Outsider | Generic terms only | "big top," "the lot" |
| 3-4 | First-of-May | Basic location terms | "back yard," "the jump," "towners" |
| 5-6 | Learning | Operational terms | "the haul," "spotted," "the wagon" |
| 7-8 | Adapting | Personnel titles | "boss hostler," "equestrian director" |
| 9-10 | Integrated | Performance terms | "the house," "sell out," "John Robinson" |
| 11-12 | Trouper | Full vocabulary | Uses terms naturally without thinking |

#### Speech Patterns
- **Early chapters:** Makes mistakes, uses wrong terms, gets corrected
- **Mid chapters:** Hesitates before using new terms, sometimes self-corrects
- **Late chapters:** Uses terms naturally, even thinks in circus vocabulary
- **Internal monologue:** Should shift from outsider perspective to insider

#### Example Dialogue Evolution

**Chapter 1:**
> "Where do the performers stay?" *(generic)*

**Chapter 5:**
> "Is the back yard off-limits to first-of-mays?" *(learning, slightly awkward)*

**Chapter 10:**
> "The house looks thin for a Saturday." *(natural, insider)*

#### Terms Tommy Would NOT Know (flags for author)
- Technical rigging terms (Rafael's domain)
- Old horse-era terminology (before his time)
- Financial/business terms (not his concern)

### RAFAEL MENDOZA (Rigger)
**Status:** Veteran trouper, 8+ seasons  
**Background:** Mexican national, Bracero program history, master rigger  
**Role:** Technical expert, Jenny's protector, Tommy's mentor

#### Vocabulary Profile

**EXPERT KNOWLEDGE:**
- All rigging terms (Spanish web, block, lunge rope, mechanic)
- Safety terminology
- Setup/teardown operations
- Weather-related decisions

**WORKING KNOWLEDGE:**
- Lot geography (back yard, padroom, clown alley)
- Performance terms (the house, spec, after show)
- Personnel hierarchy (boss hostler, equestrian director)

**CODE-SWITCHING:**
Rafael thinks and sometimes speaks in Spanish, especially:
- Under stress
- When alone with other Mexican workers
- Technical terms he learned in Spanish first
- Prayers, curses, endearments

#### Spanish/English Technical Terms

| English | Spanish | When Rafael Uses Spanish |
|---------|---------|-------------------------|
| rope | soga / cuerda | Thinking to himself |
| knot | nudo | Teaching, automatic |
| pulley/block | polea | Technical work |
| safe/secure | seguro | Checking equipment |
| careful | cuidado | Warning others |
| height | altura | Measuring, rigging |
| weight | peso | Load calculations |
| canvas | lona | Setup work |

#### Speech Patterns
- Formal with authority figures (boss hostler, equestrian director)
- Technical precision when discussing rigging
- Protective/warning tone about Jenny
- Guarded around strangers (deportation fear)
- Warm but cautious with Tommy

#### Example Dialogue

**Technical:**
> "The block needs grease. I check it twice—before matinee, before evening. Every day. No exceptions."

**Protective:**
> "The back yard is safe. Out there—" *gestures toward the lot* "—out there, you watch who watches you."

**Code-switch under stress:**
> "Cuidado with that lunge rope. You think it holds, but the fibers—they lie to you."

#### Terms Rafael Would Emphasize
- Safety equipment (mechanic, lunge rope)
- Inspection routines
- Weight limits, load bearing
- Weather impacts on rigging

### JENNY LEIGH (Aerialist)
**Status:** Featured performer, 5 seasons  
**Background:** Started as bally girl, developed solo act  
**Role:** Love interest, trapped between two worlds

#### Vocabulary Profile

**EXPERT KNOWLEDGE:**
- Performance terminology
- Aerial apparatus (Spanish web, iron jaw, mechanic)
- Spectacle/production terms
- Performer hierarchy and politics

**COMFORTABLE WITH:**
- Back yard social geography
- Show quality assessment
- Audience reading (thin house, straw house)

**DISMISSIVE OF:**
- Towner concerns
- First-of-may ignorance
- Business/money matters

#### Speech Patterns
- Confident, sometimes sharp
- Uses performer slang casually
- Dismissive of outsiders ("towners don't understand")
- Protective of circus family
- Different register with Tommy vs. Rafael vs. other performers

#### Example Dialogue

**To Tommy (teaching):**
> "The house doesn't care about your nerves. They paid for star bucks, they expect a show."

**About towners:**
> "Natives think we're all freaks or criminals. Let them think what they want."

**About her act:**
> "I worked three seasons as a bally broad before they let me touch the web. You earn your spot."

**With Rafael (intimate/professional):**
> "Check the mechanic twice tonight. The new trick—I need to know you're on the lunge rope."

#### Terms Jenny Uses Naturally
- House, blues, star bucks (audience terms)
- Spec, after show (performance structure)
- Bally broad, bally girl (her history)
- Mechanic, lunge rope (her safety)
- Towners, natives (outsider dismissal)

### CIRCUS VETERANS (Supporting Characters)
**Boss Hostler, Equestrian Director, Old Hands**

#### Vocabulary Profile
- Use ALL terms without explanation
- Reference "the old days" with horses
- Call trucks "the gas" (fading contempt)
- Know everyone by function, not name
- Speak in shorthand

#### Speech Patterns
- Terse, efficient
- Commands, not requests
- Assume everyone knows the vocabulary
- Impatient with first-of-mays
- Nostalgic about horse era

#### Example Dialogue

**Boss Hostler:**
> "Spot the cookhouse first. Gas is running hot—get it done before the canvas goes up."

**Equestrian Director:**
> "John Robinson tonight. Storm's coming. Cut the liberty horses, cut the after show. I want the haul started by ten."

**Old Hand to Tommy:**
> "First-of-may, huh? You'll learn or you'll fold. Show doesn't wait for either."

---

### TOWNERS / OUTSIDERS
**Sheriff, Local Officials, Audience Members**

#### Vocabulary Profile
- Use NO circus terminology
- Generic descriptions only
- Sometimes use wrong terms (call equestrian director "ring master")
- Suspicious or romanticized view of circus

#### Speech Patterns
- Formal or hostile (officials)
- Curious or condescending (audience)
- Use "circus people" as othering term

#### Example Dialogue

**Sheriff:**
> "You circus people keep your workers in line. We've had trouble with shows before."

**Local:**
> "My boy wants to see the animals. Where do you keep the elephants?"

## VOCABULARY CONSISTENCY RULES

### Rule 1: Status Determines Access
Higher status = more vocabulary knowledge

```
FULL VOCABULARY:
  Owner → Equestrian Director → Boss Hostler → Department Heads
  
PERFORMANCE VOCABULARY:
  Star Performers → Working Acts → Bally Girls → Band
  
OPERATIONAL VOCABULARY:
  Boss Hostler → Riggers → Roustabouts → First-of-Mays
  
NO VOCABULARY:
  Towners → Officials → Outsiders
```

### Rule 2: Experience Determines Comfort
- Veterans use terms without thinking
- Mid-career workers use terms naturally
- First-of-mays hesitate, sometimes explain
- Outsiders don't use terms at all

### Rule 3: Context Determines Register
Same character speaks differently to:
- Authority figures (formal)
- Peers (casual)
- Subordinates (commanding)
- Outsiders (guarded or dismissive)

### Rule 4: Tension Reveals Truth
Under stress, characters:
- Revert to most natural speech
- Rafael code-switches to Spanish
- Tommy might slip back to generic terms
- Veterans get more terse

## DIALOGUE AUTHENTICITY CHECKLIST

Before finalizing any dialogue, verify:

- [ ] Does this character KNOW this term based on their background?
- [ ] Would they USE this term with THIS listener?
- [ ] Is the formality level appropriate to the relationship?
- [ ] Does Tommy's vocabulary match his arc position (chapter)?
- [ ] Does Rafael code-switch appropriately under stress?
- [ ] Do towners sound like outsiders?
- [ ] Do veterans sound terse and experienced?

## RED FLAGS (Things That Would Sound Wrong)

| Wrong | Why | Right |
|-------|-----|-------|
| Tommy using "equestrian director" in Ch. 1 | He doesn't know the term yet | "the man with the whistle" |
| Rafael explaining basic terms | He's a veteran, wouldn't explain | Uses terms without definition |
| Jenny saying "the audience" | Performers say "the house" | "The house is thin tonight" |
| Boss hostler being chatty | Character is terse, efficient | Short commands only |
| Towner using "back yard" correctly | They wouldn't know the term | "where the performers stay" |
| Sheriff using "first-of-may" | Law enforcement wouldn't know | "new workers" |

## INTEGRATION NOTES FOR SPECIFIC SCENES

### Scene: Tommy's Arrival (Ch. 1)
- Tommy uses ALL generic terms
- Veterans use circus terms WITHOUT explaining
- Tommy's confusion is the reader's entry point
- Rafael might explain ONE term as introduction

### Scene: First Performance Tommy Sees
- Tommy overwhelmed by terminology he doesn't understand
- Equestrian director's whistle confuses him
- "The house" used by performers, Tommy thinks "crowd"

### Scene: Rafael Teaching Tommy
- Rafael uses technical terms, Tommy asks questions
- Opportunity to naturally define terms
- Rafael's patience shows character

### Scene: Crisis/Climax
- Everyone reverts to shortest, most essential terms
- Commands only, no explanations
- Rafael's Spanish emerges under stress
- Tommy proves he's learned by understanding without translation
